Binary Numbers

An easy way to interpret binary numbers would be to break them up into separate bits (shown below). Let's take the binary number 1101 as an example.

What's the pattern with the numbers in the above example in the denominator? As your binary number gets longer, each number exponentially increases by a factor of 2 from right to left.

Binary Numbers to Our Numbers

Now knowing the basic split-up approach, we can just add up the numbers at the bottom from left to right IF they have a 1 on top (1 for the on switch). Also helpful, you can imagine power switches with the I/O labeled on the switch, and only the machines turned on (the side with the I pressed down) produce an output.

Our Numbers to Binary Numbers

To convert numbers readable to us back into binary numbers, we need to use a different method. The method I'm showing you is a simple division method. If you have a number, say 26, you divide 26 by 2 to get 13. This would leave a remainder of 0, so put 0 in the far right of your sequence of bits. However, if you divide 13 by 2, you'd get 6 with a remainder of 1, so put 1 to the left of our last bit. Keep doing this process until you're down to 1 or 0.

Binary numbers have exponential growth with 2^x. With every new bit in a binary sequence, we add +1 to the exponent for 2^x to expand the potential of numbers we can create by another power of 2. Look how quickly it grows!

Binary System

The Binary System is used for all sorts of things other than computers as well! Some common uses include:

- data transmission over fiber optics,
- cryptography and security systems,
- some traditional communication methods like drum signals in certain cultures,
- certain biological processes where "on" or "off" states can be represented using binary logic
